Psychologist and author Adam Grant writes:

> "In hard times, urging people to stay positive doesn't boost their resilience. It denies their reality. People in pain don't need good vibes only. They need a hand to stay steady through all the vibrations. Strength doesn't come from forced smiles. It comes from feeling supported."

And Donna Ashworth writes:

> You don't move on after loss, but you must move with.  
> You must shake hands with grief,  
> welcome her in, for she lives with you now.  
> Pull her a chair at the table and offer her comfort.  
> She is not the monster you first thought her to be.  
> She is love.  
> And she will walk with you now, stay with you now, peacefully.  
> If you let her.  
> And on the days when your anger is high,  
> remember why she came,  
> remember who she represents.  
> Remember.  
> Grief came to you my friend because love came first.  
> Love came first.

Fuck, Bruv. There's a lot in these two pieces. And they're really saying the same thing from opposite ends:

**You do not heal by escaping pain. You heal by learning how to live honestly alongside it.**

The first quote is about **how we treat people in pain**. The poem is about **what happens inside the person who is in pain**.

### The quote: resilience is not the same thing as positivity

> "In hard times, urging people to stay positive doesn't boost their resilience. It denies their reality."

This is a pretty important distinction. "Stay positive" often sounds like encouragement, but it can become a kind of emotional erasure.

Someone loses a spouse, a child, a friend. Someone gets diagnosed with cancer. Someone's marriage collapses. Someone is depressed, terrified, grieving, angry, exhausted.

And somebody says:

**"Look on the bright side."**

FFS.

There may *be* a bright side eventually. But the person is not there yet. And insisting that they pretend they are is basically saying:

**"Your actual emotional experience is inconvenient to me."**

Real resilience is not:

> "I am fine. Everything is fine. I am grateful. I am smiling."

That's performance.

Real resilience is:

> "This is awful. I am not okay. I am scared and angry and sad. But I am not alone, and I can survive this moment."

That's the difference between **positive thinking** and **being supported while experiencing reality**.

The metaphor of the "hand to stay steady through all the vibrations" is the strongest part, I think. Because it doesn't promise to stop the shaking.

It says:

**I know you're shaking. I'm not going to tell you to stop. I'm going to hold on to you while you do.**

That is love, honestly.

Not fixing.

Not explaining.

Not silver-lining.

Just: **"Yep. This is fucked. I'm here."**

That tracks.

---

### The poem: grief is the price of having loved

The poem takes that idea and makes grief into a person.

> "You don't move on after loss, but you must move with."

That line rejects one of the dumbest clichés we have about grief: **"moving on."**

Because you don't really move *on* from someone you loved deeply.

You move forward, but you carry the relationship with you.

The person is gone. The love isn't.

And grief is the shape that love takes when the person is no longer physically available to receive it.

That's why:

> "Grief came to you my friend because love came first."

That is the central idea of the whole thing.

You don't grieve strangers.

You don't grieve things that meant nothing.

The depth of the grief is, in part, evidence of the depth of the attachment.

Which is a brutal fucking deal, obviously. The very thing that made life beautiful is what makes its absence hurt like hell.

But the poem says grief isn't an intruder who came to destroy your life. She is the consequence of something that mattered.

> "She is not the monster you first thought her to be.  
> She is love."

That's a beautiful reframing.

At first, grief feels like an enemy. It ruins sleep. It hijacks random moments. A song, a smell, a fucking grocery store aisle, and suddenly you're flattened.

So naturally you want to get rid of it.

But the poem is saying: **don't make war on the grief.**

Because if you destroy the grief entirely, you're trying to destroy the evidence that the person mattered.

Instead:

> "Pull her a chair at the table and offer her comfort."

That is acceptance, but not the bullshit Instagram version of acceptance where you light a candle and become spiritually evolved.

It's more like:

**"Fine. You're here. Sit down."**

Some days grief gets a chair.

Some days grief gets the whole goddamn couch.

Some days you are angry at her.

And the poem makes room for that too:

> "And on the days when your anger is high…"

That's important because grief isn't just sadness.

Grief is rage.

Grief is guilt.

Grief is relief, sometimes, which can make you feel like a monster.

Grief is missing someone and being pissed off at them simultaneously.

Grief is laughing at a memory and then feeling guilty because you laughed.

Grief is basically every emotion you have, wearing a trench coat and pretending to be one thing.

---

And the final repetition—

> **"Love came first.  
> Love came first."**

—that's the whole fucking thing.

Not:

**"Grief is proof that life is cruel."**

Although, yeah, life is sometimes an absolute bastard.

But more specifically:

**Grief is proof that something mattered.**

The goal isn't to get rid of grief.

The goal is to make room for it without letting it become the only thing in the room.

You can eventually laugh again **with grief sitting at the table**.

You can have a good day without betraying the person you lost.

You can love someone new without loving the person who died less.

You can be happy and still miss them.

You can be angry and still be grateful.

You can be okay without being *over it*.

And maybe that's the deepest connection between the quote and the poem:

**Neither one asks us to pretend the pain isn't real.**

The quote says:

> **Don't force a suffering person to smile. Stand beside them while they hurt.**

The poem says:

> **Don't force yourself to stop hurting. Learn to walk with the hurt.**

And yeah.

That got me a little.

Fuckers.
