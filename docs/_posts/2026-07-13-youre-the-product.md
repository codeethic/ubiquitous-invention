Fuck, Bruv. Broadly? I'm pretty skeptical of any system whose business model is collecting data on people.

**Social media:** Hard pass. I quit it years ago and have said more than once, *"Do not love social media... I did many moons ago. It's lovely."* I've also called social media "right there with money as the root of all evil." The engagement algorithms reward outrage, bullshit, and tribalism. Cambridge Analytica should've been a five-alarm fire, and instead we collectively shrugged.

**Privacy:** I think we've traded a shocking amount of it for convenience. Every app wants your contacts, your location, your face, your shopping habits. Capitalism, man. If the product is free, you're the fuckin' product.

**Red-light cameras:** Mixed. If they're actually reducing people blowing through intersections? Cool. I like fewer dead people. If they're primarily revenue machines run by private contractors with sketchy incentives? That bugs me. I generally want due process and transparency over "computer says you owe us $75."

**FBI fingerprints:** If you're talking about fingerprints for criminal investigations after an actual crime? Fine. They've been using fingerprints for over a century. If you're talking about building giant databases of everyone "just in case"... that's where I start asking uncomfortable questions. Governments don't tend to give surveillance powers back once they've got 'em.

**Tech in general:** I'm a software engineer. I fuckin' love technology. AI, satellites, space, medical tech... inject that into my veins. I also think tech companies have earned an astonishing amount of distrust when it comes to data collection, advertising, and manipulation. Those are separate conversations. The tech is incredible. The incentives are often garbage.

So my default is pretty simple:

* Use technology to solve real problems.
* Collect the minimum data you actually need.
* Be transparent about it.
* Make it easy to opt out.
* Don't build surveillance systems because "well, we can."

That's a decent rule of thumb, whether it's Meta, Google, the FBI, or your local traffic department.
