Yeep. Here's one that actually made me smile.

A few years ago, researchers noticed that a species of sea otter off the California coast had started doing something nobody taught them. They were wrapping injured pups in strands of kelp before leaving them floating while they dove for food. Kelp acts like an anchor, so the pups wouldn't drift away in the current.

The cool part wasn't just the behavior—it spread.

Young otters watched older females do it and copied them. Then those pups grew up and taught the next generation. Scientists realized they were watching culture happen in real time: knowledge being passed from one generation to the next, not through instinct, but by learning.

Bruv, I don't know why that gets me.

A tired mom otter figuring out a better way to keep her kid safe. Another otter seeing it and thinking, "Huh. That's smart." Then twenty years later, a baby is alive because of an idea some other otter had before it was born.

The world spends so much time reminding us how clever we are at destroying shit that it's easy to forget evolution also produced little floating fuzzballs inventing babysitting hacks.

That tracks.

We're not the only species that learns from each other. We're just the loudest about it. And somewhere off the California coast, a chain of tiny acts of care has been quietly continuing because one otter solved a problem and another paid attention.

Fuckin' wholesome.
